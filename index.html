Freedom as My First Language

Lessons in Liberation (Complete Edition)

Written by: Logan Race

Part I: Freedom is My First Language

Freedom (n.) /the lived rebellion of oppression/

Ellendale, Delaware
circa 2000

Self-Accepted

I grew up in a double-wide trailer in Ellendale, Delaware. The outside looked like a junkyard—patchy grass, nowhere to park, rust in the air.

But inside? It was a house lined with Black brilliance. Bookshelves sagging under the weight of Baldwin and bell hooks, Zora and Assata. Big books, small books, red books, blue books. It looked like we were preparing for a war, and the books were our weapons.

Denise—mother, my first teacher—kept us surrounded by literature. Reading wasn't optional. It wasn't escape. It was inheritance.

That's why that old, tired saying never made sense to me:

"If you ever wanted to hide something from a nigger, put it in a book."

To me, that meant something was being hidden.

But if they really wanted to hide it,
why put it somewhere so public?
Made no sense—
unless they never expected us to read it.

Like the Constitution, or the Bill of Rights.
Warm, sleepy, monotone, nasally voice teaching
Foundational knowledge we all should know
But fell asleep during the class that taught it.

So, my child-brain took it as a challenge:

"Cool. I'll just read every book in the library."

Matilda but Black.
Same fluorescent lights buzzed,
Cheap sneakers that squeaked on linoleum
but no Ms. Honey.
Just me, dusty books, and a dare.

I thought I had outsmarted the oh-so-powerful white man.
White people are weird.

So, I read.
Consuming what they called knowledge
and choking on
the limits in every line.

Nat Turner Play

In third grade, I wrote a play about Harriet Tubman leading people to freedom. It won the local history competition.

In fifth grade, I did it again—a play about Nat Turner's rebellion. And I won again, for writing a story about a deadly slave rebellion. Looking back... wild.

A whole-ass Nat Turner rebellion play? In a mostly white school?

The well-meaning liberals ate it up like Sunday dinner. They never asked about the irony.

Or maybe they did. Either way, I didn't flinch.

Curated Knowledge

From first through twelfth grade, I devoured every book I could.
I read more than anyone in school history.
My name stayed on a printout in the library.
At a school full of white doctor children—
the little Black girl had read the most books.

I liked being that girl, the girl who reads

My mom supported my dream of being a reader, a thinker.
But she made one decision that shaped me forever:
She curated my knowledge.

Nearly every book I read was by someone Black.
That wasn't an accident.
Dr. Seuss was allowed.
Oh, and A Child Called It.
My 8th grade teacher said I might like it.

I wonder why she thought that.

Outside of Seuss and that one book, every author was Black.
Picture books. Novels. All of it.
It wasn't until Harry Potter dropped—summer before 9th grade—
that I stepped outside that curated world.

Like the boy in It, and like Harry,
always hearing the laughing
but never doing the laughing—
I knew what it meant to wait for better days.

But I wasn't waiting for magic.
Just... better.
Falling in a fit of giggles
with a bestie.

Still, I hadn't chosen a single book by a non-Black author.
No Judy Blume. No Hardy Boys. No Roald Dahl.
I didn't learn about Shakespeare, the Greeks, or the Romans until college.

Every time the world tried to enforce whiteness on me,
my mother countered it with Blackness.

I once came home from school, after learning we all came from somewhere—
and that the only true Americans were the Natives.
I told my mom, and I asked her what I was mixed with.

With a slight raise of the eyebrow, and an amused hmph, she answered:
"Black and Blacker. If you're mixed, it's with Black and Blacker."
"Yea but my real dad..."
"Joe is your real dad."
"But my real dad isn't Black."
"Oh yea? Then what the hell is he?"

I shrugged.
He wasn't the same color as Denise and me.

She then told me to ask my teacher what Black was mixed with.
"See if she'll tell you the truth," she muttered.

The people in my coloring books were always brown.

She didn't just tell me I was Black.
She made sure I knew it meant power.
